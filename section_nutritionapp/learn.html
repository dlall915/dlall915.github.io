Macronutrient

- a substance required in relatively large amounts by living organisms, in particular.

  • Carbohydrates: These should make up 45 to 65 percent of your daily calories and are made up of starch, sugar, and fiber.
  • Proteins: These should make up 10 to 35 percent of your daily calories . Their role in the body includes building, maintaining, and repairing body tissue. There are other roles too, including hormone production and in aiding our immune system.
  • Fats: These should make up about 20 percent of your daily calories. Despite the misconceptions about fats, they play important roles in our body. Some of these are insulation to keep us warm and protect our organs, promoting growth and development, and helping to digest certain vitamins.

    • *As you may know, alcohol also provides us with calories, but is not considered a macronutrient since we do not need it for survival.

    Micronutrient

    - a chemical element or substance required in trace amounts for the normal growth and development of living organisms.

  • Vitamins: These can be split into two categories, fat-soluble vitamins (A and D for example) and water-soluble vitamins (Bs and C for example). Water-soluble vitamins require a steady daily intake from a combinations of food and supplements as your body does not store most of them while fat-soluble vitamins are stored in your body tissue.
  • Vitamin A: Keeps tissue and skin healthy. Plays an important role in bone growth. Also aids with vision in dim light. The most common type of Vitamin A is beta-carotene, which is responsible for the orange pigment in many foods such as carrots, pumpkin, sweet potatoes, and cantaloupe.

  • Vitamin D: Plays a role in your nerve, muscle, and immune systems. It also helps your body absorb calcium. Your body can actually form vitamin D naturally when you are out in the sunlight. Some foods that can also provide you with vitamin D are tuna, eggs (yolk), and cod liver oil.
  • Vitamin E: Vital to your immune system and cell protection. It's antioxidant properties are what make this vitamin so popular for skin care. Some sources are almonds, peanuts, and various vegetable oils.

    Calorie

    Calories are a measure of energy. This energy fuels our body the way gasoline fuels a car.

    Antioxidant

    Antioxidants protect the body from damage caused by harmful molecules called free radicals. You are exposed to free radicals by normal processes that take place in your body, the breaking down of certain medicines, and pollution.

    CLOSE X

    Supplement

    Something that completes or enhances something else when added to it. For example, using a multivitamin may supplement your vitamin C for the day if you are only getting 50% of the recommended amount through your diet.
